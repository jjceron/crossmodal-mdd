\section{Materials and Methods}
\label{sec:materials&methods}

This section describes the methodological framework and experimental components employed in this study. First, the multimodal depression dataset and its preprocessing pipeline are introduced. Subsequently, the unimodal feature extractors and the proposed cross-modal fusion architecture are presented.

\subsection{Dataset and Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathcal{X}_s, \mathcal{Y}_s, y_s)\}_{s=1}^{N}$ denote the multimodal dataset, where $\mathcal{X}_s \subset \mathbb{R}^{C \times T_{\mathrm{eeg}}}$ is the set of EEG recording windows for subject $s$ with $C = 64$ channels and $T_{\mathrm{eeg}} = 500$ temporal samples, $\mathcal{Y}_s \subset \mathbb{R}^{M \times T_{\mathrm{aud}}}$ is the corresponding set of Mel-spectrogram windows with $M = 64$ bands and $T_{\mathrm{aud}} = 200$ temporal frames, and $y_s \in \{0, 1\}$ is the clinical label (0: healthy control, 1: major depressive disorder). Of the $N^{\mathrm{eeg}} = 53$ subjects with EEG recordings (24 MDD, 29 HC) and $N^{\mathrm{aud}} = 52$ subjects with audio recordings (23 MDD, 29 HC), a verified one-to-one subject-level mapping yields a paired subset of $N^{\mathrm{paired}} = 38$ subjects (17 MDD, 21 HC) possessing both modalities. This mapping guarantees that every EEG identifier corresponds to exactly one audio identifier and vice versa, a property that underwrites the validity of subject-grouped cross-validation in Section~\ref{subsec:nested_cv}. The learning objective is subject-level binary classification; temporal windows are treated as internal evidence units and never as independent samples.

\subsection{Signal Acquisition and Preprocessing}

Resting-state EEG signals were acquired from $N^{\mathrm{eeg}} = 53$ participants using a 128-channel HydroCel Geodesic Sensor Net at a sampling rate of 250 Hz. The raw recordings $\mathcal{X}_s$ were processed through the following pipeline.

First, a band-pass FIR filter with passband 0.5--60 Hz was applied, followed by a notch filter at 50 Hz to remove power-line interference. The first $C = 64$ channels (indices 0--63) were retained. This selection is fixed and independent of the data; an alternative channel-ranking procedure based on FTSM~\cite{} exists in the codebase but was not used in any reported experiment.

Average referencing was computed \emph{after} channel selection over the retained $C = 64$ channels. For the selected subset with signal matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times T_{\mathrm{raw}}}$, the average-referenced signal is:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_{\mathrm{ref}}[c, t] = \mathbf{X}[c, t] - \frac{1}{64} \sum_{c'=1}^{64} \mathbf{X}[c', t],
\label{eq:avg_ref}
\end{equation}

where the reference is computed from the retained subset only.

The preprocessed signal was segmented into windows of length $T_{\mathrm{eeg}} = 500$ samples (2 seconds at 250 Hz) with a stride of 250 samples (50\% overlap). Given a recording of $T_{\mathrm{raw}}$ samples, the number of windows generated by the sliding window is:

\begin{equation}
n_{\mathrm{wins}} = \left\lfloor \frac{T_{\mathrm{raw}} - T_{\mathrm{eeg}}}{\mathrm{stride}} \right\rfloor + 1,
\label{eq:n_windows}
\end{equation}

with $\mathrm{stride} = 250$. All resulting windows were retained at the caching stage.

Two distinct window-selection mechanisms operate at different stages of the pipeline. During \emph{backbone training}, if a subject contributes more than $K_{\max} = 200$ windows, a random subset of exactly $K_{\max}$ windows is drawn without replacement using a per-subject deterministic seed ($\mathrm{seed} = \mathrm{RANDOM\_STATE} + \mathrm{subject\_index}$). During \emph{fusion feature extraction}, window selection is deterministic: an evenly spaced subset of $K_{\max}$ indices is selected via linear interpolation across the full window sequence:

\begin{equation}
i_j = \left\lfloor \frac{j \cdot (n - 1)}{K_{\max} - 1} \right\rfloor, \quad j = 0, \dots, K_{\max} - 1,
\label{eq:deterministic_windows}
\end{equation}

for $n > K_{\max}$, ensuring uniform temporal coverage without stochastic variation between runs.

Each window $\mathbf{w}_{s,k}^{(e)} \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times 500}$ is independently $z$-score normalized:

\begin{equation}
\bar{\mathbf{w}}_{s,k}^{(e)} = \frac{\mathbf{w}_{s,k}^{(e)} - \mu(\mathbf{w}_{s,k}^{(e)})}{\sigma(\mathbf{w}_{s,k}^{(e)}) + \varepsilon},
\quad \varepsilon = 10^{-8},
\label{eq:zscore_window}
\end{equation}

where $\mu(\cdot)$ and $\sigma(\cdot)$ are computed over all elements of the window jointly (flattened across channel and time dimensions).

Audio recordings from $N^{\mathrm{aud}} = 52$ participants were acquired at 44.1 kHz, 24-bit, single channel, comprising five speech-task categories from the MODMA clinical protocol. All recordings per subject were concatenated into a single audio stream before any spectral transform.

The concatenated stream was resampled from 44.1 kHz to 16 kHz and amplitude-normalized by converting integer PCM samples to float. The short-time Fourier transform (STFT) was applied with $\mathrm{n\_fft} = 1024$, $\mathrm{hop\_length} = 160$, and a Hann window, producing a complex spectrogram per frame. The power spectrogram was then mapped to $M = 64$ Mel-frequency bands via the standard Mel filterbank:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Mel}(f) = 2595 \cdot \log_{10}\left(1 + \frac{f}{700}\right),
\label{eq:mel_scale}
\end{equation}

with filterbank spanning $f_{\min} = 20$ Hz to $f_{\max} = 8000$ Hz. The resulting Mel-power spectrogram is:

\begin{equation}
X_{\mathrm{mel}}[m, k] = \sum_{f} |\mathrm{STFT}(x)[f, k]|^2 \cdot H_m[f],
\label{eq:mel_spectrogram}
\end{equation}

where $H_m$ is the $m$-th triangular Mel filter and $k$ indexes time frames. The power spectrogram was converted to decibel scale:

\begin{equation}
X_{\mathrm{dB}} = 10 \cdot \log_{10}\big(\max(X_{\mathrm{mel}}, \varepsilon)\big),
\label{eq:db_scale}
\end{equation}

and clipped to $[\max(X_{\mathrm{dB}}) - 80, \max(X_{\mathrm{dB}})]$ (i.e., $\mathrm{top\_dB} = 80$).

The Mel-spectrogram was segmented into windows of $T_{\mathrm{aud}} = 200$ frames (approximately 2 seconds at the given hop length) with 50\% overlap, following Eq.~\eqref{eq:n_windows} with $\mathrm{stride} = 100$ frames.

If the concatenated-and-windowed sequence exceeded $K_{\max}$ windows per subject, a random subset of exactly $K_{\max}$ windows was drawn without replacement using a fixed global seed (identical for every subject). This selection was applied once at the caching stage, before any cross-validation split and independently of subject labels.

Each audio window $\mathbf{w}_{s,k}^{(a)} \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times 200}$ was $z$-score normalized analogously to Eq.~\eqref{eq:zscore_window}, yielding $\bar{\mathbf{w}}_{s,k}^{(a)}$.

\subsection{Unimodal Backbone Feature Extractors}

Let $\Phi_{\theta} : \mathbb{R}^{C \times T_{\mathrm{eeg}}} \to \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathrm{eeg}}}$ denote the EEG feature extractor, implemented as a deep convolutional architecture based on DeepConvNet~\cite{schirrmeister2017deep} with $|\theta| = 34\,361$ trainable parameters and output dimensionality $D_{\mathrm{eeg}} = 128$. The network comprises four convolutional blocks: the first two apply temporal convolutions along the time axis with filter sizes 25 and 15, each followed by batch normalization, ELU activation, max-pooling (stride 2), and dropout (rate 0.5). The remaining blocks progressively reduce the temporal resolution while preserving the channel dimension. For a given normalized EEG window $\bar{\mathbf{w}}_{s,k}^{(e)}$, the backbone produces a feature vector:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_{s,k}^{(e)} = \Phi_{\theta}(\bar{\mathbf{w}}_{s,k}^{(e)}) \in \mathbb{R}^{128}.
\label{eq:eeg_backbone}
\end{equation}

Let $\Psi_{\phi} : \mathbb{R}^{M \times T_{\mathrm{aud}}} \to \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathrm{aud}}}$ denote the audio feature extractor, implemented as a shallow convolutional architecture based on ShallowConvNet~\cite{schirrmeister2017deep} with $|\phi| = 37\,849$ parameters and output dimensionality $D_{\mathrm{aud}} = 576$. The network applies a temporal convolution (kernel 13), followed by a spatial convolution across Mel bands, squaring, mean pooling, and logarithmic compression. For a given normalized audio window $\bar{\mathbf{w}}_{s,k}^{(a)}$, the encoder yields:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_{s,k}^{(a)} = \Psi_{\phi}(\bar{\mathbf{w}}_{s,k}^{(a)}) \in \mathbb{R}^{576}.
\label{eq:aud_backbone}
\end{equation}

Both backbones are trained independently with binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss, label smoothing ($\tilde{y} = y \times 0.95 + 0.025$), and early stopping (patience 15) on a subject-grouped inner validation split. After training, $\Phi_{\theta}$ and $\Psi_{\phi}$ are frozen; their weights are not updated during fusion training to prevent label leakage across the nested cross-validation folds.

\subsection{Cross-Modal Fusion Architecture}

Given a paired subject with $K$ windows, the frozen backbones produce feature matrices $\mathbf{H}^{(e)} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times 128}$ and $\mathbf{H}^{(a)} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times 576}$, where the $k$-th row corresponds to $\mathbf{h}_{s,k}^{(e)}$ and $\mathbf{h}_{s,k}^{(a)}$ respectively. Each modality is projected to a common hidden dimension $D = 64$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}^{(e)} = \mathbf{H}^{(e)} \mathbf{W}^{(e)}, \qquad
\mathbf{Z}^{(a)} = \mathbf{H}^{(a)} \mathbf{W}^{(a)},
\label{eq:projections}
\end{equation}

with $\mathbf{W}^{(e)} \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 64}$ and $\mathbf{W}^{(a)} \in \mathbb{R}^{576 \times 64}$. 

Bidirectional cross-modal multi-head attention (MHA) operates on the projected window sequences:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{A}^{(e)} &= \mathrm{MHA}\big(\mathbf{Q}^{(e)}, \mathbf{K}^{(a)}, \mathbf{V}^{(a)}\big), \\
\mathbf{A}^{(a)} &= \mathrm{MHA}\big(\mathbf{Q}^{(a)}, \mathbf{K}^{(e)}, \mathbf{V}^{(e)}\big),
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:cross_attention}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{Q}^{(e)} = \mathbf{Z}^{(e)}$, $\mathbf{K}^{(a)} = \mathbf{V}^{(a)} = \mathbf{Z}^{(a)}$, and symmetrically for the audio-to-EEG direction. The MHA mechanism follows~\cite{vaswani2017attention} with $H = 2$ heads:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{MHA}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \bigoplus_{h=1}^{H} \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{W}_h^Q (\mathbf{K}\mathbf{W}_h^K)^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) \mathbf{V} \mathbf{W}_h^V,
\label{eq:mha}
\end{equation}

with per-head dimension $d_h = D / H = 32$. The attended representations are combined via residual addition and RMS normalization:

\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\mathbf{Z}}^{(e)} = \mathrm{RMS}\big(\mathbf{Z}^{(e)} + \mathbf{A}^{(e)}\big), \quad
\widetilde{\mathbf{Z}}^{(a)} = \mathrm{RMS}\big(\mathbf{Z}^{(a)} + \mathbf{A}^{(a)}\big),
\label{eq:cross_residual}
\end{equation}

and fused through element-wise averaging: $\widetilde{\mathbf{Z}} = \frac{1}{2}(\widetilde{\mathbf{Z}}^{(e)} + \widetilde{\mathbf{Z}}^{(a)})$.

A post-fusion self-attention layer (1 layer, 4 heads) refines the joint representation $\widetilde{\mathbf{Z}}$ across the window dimension:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}^{\star} = \mathrm{SelfAttn}(\widetilde{\mathbf{Z}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times 64}.
\label{eq:self_attn}
\end{equation}

The subject-level logit is obtained by mean-pooling the window representations followed by a two-layer MLP classifier with hidden dimension 64 and dropout rate 0.7:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = \sigma\!\left( \mathrm{MLP}\!\left( \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathbf{z}_k^{\star} \right) \right) \in [0, 1],
\label{eq:classification}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma(\cdot)$ denotes the sigmoid function. Loss is BCE with dynamic positive weight $w_{\mathrm{pos}} = n_{\mathrm{HC}} / n_{\mathrm{MDD}}$ computed per training fold after the subject partition, and label smoothing $\tilde{y} = y \times 0.95 + 0.025$.
